The Complete Guide to Sourdough Bread Baking

By Maria Gonzalez | Updated February 2025

Sourdough bread baking is one of the most rewarding culinary skills you can develop. There is something deeply satisfying about combining just three simple ingredients, flour, water, and salt, and transforming them into a beautifully crusty, tangy loaf through the power of wild fermentation. Unlike commercial bread that relies on instant yeast for a quick rise, sourdough uses a living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria that develops complex flavors over a long, slow fermentation process.

Building Your Starter

The foundation of all sourdough baking is the starter, also known as a levain or mother dough. A sourdough starter is a simple mixture of flour and water that captures wild yeast and beneficial bacteria from the environment. Creating a starter from scratch typically takes between seven and fourteen days, during which you feed it daily with fresh flour and water, discarding a portion each time to maintain a manageable volume. The process requires patience, but the reward is a living culture that can be maintained indefinitely with regular feeding.

To begin your starter, combine equal parts by weight of whole wheat flour and lukewarm water in a clean glass jar. Whole wheat flour contains more wild yeast and nutrients than white flour, giving your starter a head start. Cover loosely and let it sit at room temperature, ideally around 75 degrees Fahrenheit. After 24 hours, you may see some bubbles forming, which is a good sign. Discard half the mixture and feed with fresh flour and water. Repeat this process daily, and within a week or two, your starter should be reliably doubling in volume within four to six hours of feeding.

The Flour Matters

The choice of flour has a profound impact on the character of your sourdough bread. Bread flour, with its higher protein content of around 12 to 14 percent, produces a stronger gluten network that results in a taller, more open crumb. All-purpose flour works well for softer, more tender loaves. Whole wheat and rye flours add flavor complexity and nutritional value but also absorb more water and can make the dough stickier and more challenging to handle. Many experienced bakers use a blend of flours to achieve their desired balance of flavor, texture, and workability.

Freshly milled flour is increasingly popular among serious home bakers. Stone-ground flour retains more of the grain's natural oils, enzymes, and flavor compounds compared to commercially roller-milled flour. If you have access to a home grain mill, even incorporating a small percentage of freshly milled grain into your dough can make a noticeable difference in flavor. Heritage wheat varieties like Turkey Red, Red Fife, and Sonora offer distinctive flavors that are well worth seeking out from specialty mills.

Mixing and Fermentation

The mixing process for sourdough differs from conventional bread making. Most sourdough recipes begin with an autolyse, a period of 30 minutes to several hours during which the flour and water are mixed together and allowed to rest before the starter and salt are added. This resting period allows the flour to fully hydrate and begins the development of gluten without any mechanical input. After the autolyse, the starter is incorporated, followed by the salt, which strengthens the gluten but also slows fermentation.

Bulk fermentation is where the magic happens. During this phase, which typically lasts four to eight hours at room temperature, the wild yeast in your starter produces carbon dioxide that inflates the dough, while the lactic acid bacteria generate the organic acids responsible for sourdough's characteristic tang. During bulk fermentation, you will perform a series of stretch and folds every 30 to 60 minutes. These gentle manipulations develop gluten strength and help distribute the gases evenly throughout the dough without the aggressive kneading required for conventional bread.

Shaping and Baking

Shaping is one of the most skill-dependent aspects of sourdough baking. The goal is to create surface tension on the outside of the dough while maintaining the gas structure developed during fermentation. For a round boule, you will use a series of folds and a final tightening motion on an unfloured surface to build tension. For an oval batard, the technique involves a letter fold followed by a rolling motion. Proper shaping takes practice, but the improvement in your bread's appearance and oven spring will be dramatic as your technique develops.

After shaping, the dough typically undergoes a final proof in the refrigerator overnight. This cold retard serves multiple purposes: it slows the fermentation, allowing more complex flavors to develop; it firms up the dough, making it easier to score and load into the oven; and it provides scheduling flexibility, letting you bake fresh bread in the morning without waking up at an unreasonable hour. Score the chilled dough with a razor blade or lame just before baking. The scores control where the bread expands in the oven and create the distinctive pattern that is both functional and decorative.

Baking sourdough requires high heat and steam. Preheat your oven to 450 to 500 degrees Fahrenheit with a Dutch oven inside. The enclosed environment of the Dutch oven traps steam released by the dough, keeping the crust pliable during the initial phase of baking and allowing maximum oven spring. After 20 minutes, remove the lid to allow the crust to develop its deep golden-brown color. The total baking time is typically 40 to 50 minutes, depending on the size of your loaf and the characteristics of your oven.

The hardest part of sourdough baking might just be waiting for the bread to cool. A freshly baked loaf needs at least an hour, preferably two, to finish its internal cooking and for the crumb structure to set. Cutting into a loaf too early results in a gummy interior. Once cooled, sourdough bread keeps well for several days at room temperature, wrapped in a clean kitchen towel or stored cut-side down on a cutting board. The natural acidity of sourdough acts as a preservative, giving it a longer shelf life than commercially yeasted bread.
